Acute Rheumatic Fever

"An autoimmune inflammatory response to Group A Streptococcus infection – can lead to rheumatic heart disease." – Pillitteri / WHO

Rheumatic Fever Like You Are 5 Years Old

🧸 Imagine your body has tiny soldiers (immune system) that fight bad germs.

• When you get a strep throat (a sore throat caused by a specific germ), your soldiers make weapons (antibodies) to fight it.
• Sometimes, the weapons get confused and attack your own body – especially your heart, joints, brain, and skin.
• This confusion causes rheumatic fever – painful, swollen joints (arthritis), a racing heart (carditis), strange jerky movements (chorea), pink rash, and lumps under the skin.
• The most serious part is heart damage (rheumatic heart disease), which can make the heart valves leaky or tight.

🩺 The good news: treating strep throat quickly with antibiotics can prevent rheumatic fever!

Definition & Epidemiology

Acute rheumatic fever (ARF) – an autoimmune, inflammatory reaction to Group A streptococcal (Streptococcus pyogenes) pharyngitis. It affects the heart, joints, central nervous system, and skin. Rheumatic heart disease (RHD) is the chronic sequelae of valvular damage.

📊 Global (WHO)
• Over 300,000 deaths/year from RHD.
• Most common in children 5‑14 years.
• Highest incidence in low‑ and middle‑income countries.
• ARF incidence: 5‑200 per 100,000 population.
🇵🇭 Philippines (DOH, PHA)
• RHD is a leading cause of cardiovascular morbidity in young Filipinos.
• Estimated prevalence of RHD: 2‑5 per 1,000 school‑age children.
• Many cases present late with heart failure.
• Limited access to secondary prophylaxis in remote areas.
⚠️ Key point: The first episode of ARF typically occurs 1‑5 weeks after untreated or inadequately treated Group A streptococcal pharyngitis.

Etiology & Pathophysiology

🦠 Etiologic agent
• Group A β‑hemolytic Streptococcus (Streptococcus pyogenes).
• Only pharyngeal infections (strep throat) trigger ARF, not skin infections (impetigo).
• M protein serotypes (particularly types 1, 3, 5, 6, 14, 18, 24).
🔬 Pathophysiology (molecular mimicry)
• Antibodies against streptococcal M protein cross‑react with host tissues: cardiac myosin, valve glycoproteins, synovium, neuronal cells (caudate nucleus).
• Aschoff bodies (perivascular granulomas) in myocardium.
• Pancarditis (endocardium, myocardium, pericardium) → chronic valvular thickening, fusion, regurgitation/stenosis.
📌 Pillitteri note: "The most important risk factor for first‑time ARF is a previous episode of ARF (recurrence risk high)."

Clinical Features (Jones Criteria)

🔴 Major Jones criteria (5)
1. Carditis (50‑70%) – pancarditis, murmur (mitral regurgitation, aortic regurgitation), cardiomegaly, pericarditis, heart failure.
2. Polyarthritis (70‑75%) – migratory, asymmetric, large joints (knees, ankles, elbows, wrists). Pain out of proportion to swelling.
3. Chorea (Sydenham chorea) (10‑30%) – involuntary, purposeless movements, emotional lability, “milkmaid’s grip”. Late manifestation (months after infection).
4. Erythema marginatum (rare) – pink, non‑pruritic, serpiginous rash on trunk/extensors.
5. Subcutaneous nodules (rare) – small, painless nodules on extensor surfaces, associated with severe carditis.
🟡 Minor Jones criteria
• Fever (≥38°C)
• Arthralgia (if arthritis not present)
• Elevated acute phase reactants: ESR ≥60 mm/hr, CRP ≥30 mg/L
• Prolonged PR interval on ECG (first‑degree heart block)
📌 Diagnosis (Modified Jones criteria): 2 major criteria OR 1 major + 2 minor criteria, PLUS evidence of antecedent Group A strep infection (positive throat culture, rapid antigen, or elevated ASO/anti‑DNase B).

Diagnosis & Tests

  • Evidence of preceding GAS infection: • Throat culture or rapid antigen test.
    • Elevated streptococcal antibody titers: ASO (antistreptolysin O) rises 1‑3 weeks after infection, peaks at 4‑6 weeks. Anti‑DNase B (more sensitive in skin infections).
  • Acute phase reactants: • CRP, ESR – elevated in inflammation, non‑specific but required as minor criteria.
  • Cardiac evaluation: • ECG: prolonged PR interval (first‑degree AV block), other arrhythmias.
    • Echocardiography (Doppler) – essential to detect subclinical carditis, valvular regurgitation/stenosis.
  • Other tests: • Anti‑DNAse B, ASO titers (serial).
🔍 Pillitteri pearl: "Echocardiography should be performed in all suspected ARF cases, even if no murmur, to detect subclinical valvulitis."

Management (Acute & Secondary Prophylaxis)

💊 Acute ARF treatment
• Eradicate GAS: penicillin or amoxicillin (even if culture negative after clinical diagnosis).
• Arthritis/arthralgia: aspirin (50‑70 mg/kg/day) or naproxen.
• Carditis with heart failure: prednisone (2 mg/kg/day), diuretics, ACE inhibitors, digoxin.
• Chorea: haloperidol, valproate, or carbamazepine; supportive care.
• Bed rest during active carditis.
🛡️ Secondary prophylaxis (prevent recurrence)
• Benzathine penicillin G IM every 21‑28 days – gold standard.
• Duration:
– No carditis: 5 years or until age 21 (whichever longer).
– Carditis without residual RHD: 10 years or until age 25.
– Severe RHD: lifelong (at least 40 years).
• Oral penicillin or erythromycin if IM not possible.
📌 Primary prevention (treat strep throat): Benzathine penicillin G IM single dose (or oral penicillin V for 10 days). Prevents first episode of ARF.

Nursing Care & Complications

👩‍⚕️ Nursing interventions
• Administer prescribed medications (aspirin, steroids, antibiotics).
• Monitor cardiac status: heart sounds for murmurs, vital signs, signs of heart failure (dyspnea, edema, hepatomegaly).
• Promote bed rest during active carditis.
• Protect joints (support splints, comfort measures).
• For chorea: provide calm environment, padded bed rails, assist with feeding.
• Educate family on long‑term prophylaxis schedule and importance of follow‑up echocardiograms.
⚠️ Complications
• Rheumatic heart disease (RHD) – mitral stenosis most common (followed by aortic regurgitation).
• Heart failure (pulmonary edema, hepatomegaly).
• Atrial fibrillation (due to mitral stenosis).
• Infective endocarditis (prophylaxis needed for some procedures in high‑risk RHD).
• Recurrences (each recurrence increases risk of cardiac damage).
📞 Teach family red flags: Shortness of breath, orthopnea, palpitations, chest pain, new murmur, joint swelling, fever – report immediately.

Prevention & Health Promotion

  • Primary prevention (prevent first ARF): • Prompt diagnosis and adequate treatment of Group A streptococcal pharyngitis with penicillin or amoxicillin for 10 days.
    • Public health campaigns for sore throat management in high‑risk communities.
    • School‑based screening programs for RHD in endemic areas.
  • Secondary prevention (prevent recurrence): • Adherence to long‑term benzathine penicillin G IM injections.
    • Use of a national RHD register (Philippine RHD Registry).
    • Patient education on the importance of prophylaxis even without symptoms.
  • Health promotion: • Improve living conditions, reduce crowding.
    • Promote timely healthcare seeking for sore throat.
    • Educate parents about the link between strep throat and heart disease.
📌 DOH Philippines program: The Philippine RHD Control Program – aims to provide benzathine penicillin to all diagnosed RHD patients free of charge, improve registry and follow‑up.
